200 million adults in the rich Western world are functionally illiterate. We showed previously what this meant: They cannot read a sign, figure out where to put their Date of Birth on a form,compute calories on a box of cookies or read menus. Without some degree of literacy and numeracy, they are completely locked-out from participating in their societies. 

Throughout the 20th century, similar numbers have been used to energize societies, teachers, and entire educational systems with a bellicose cry of a “War on Illiteracy.” But here is what is difficult this time around: We really don’t know anymore what such a war would imply.

To see where the problem lies, Figure 1, shows the growth in education budgets per capita from 1970 to 2020 in multiple OECD countries. Each grey line is a separate country (different lengths in the data series implies that they start and stop at different places) and the dark line is an average. What is clear is that education budgets *have dramatically increased even as adult illiteracy has barely budged–or become worse.* Think about that: At a time of the most exhilarating and rapid technological change that our societies have ever seen, and these are changes that *democratized knowledge*, put what the world knew at our fingertips, it became more and more costly to educate a child sufficiently so that they would be barely literate as an adult.

Economists call this the great productivity decline in education, and it remains one of the most underappreciated developments of the late 20th century: The West is becoming increasingly illiterate, not because governments are spending less money on education or because education is becoming less of a priority. No, they are becoming increasingly illiterate *despite* a substantial increase in what these countries spend on educating its citizens.

Let’s dive a bit deeper into what is going on. Think about a population with just two age-groups: The young, and the old. Then, the extent of literacy in the population can be thought of as a weighted average of literacy among the young and among the old. That is, literacy in the population is: % of the population that is young \* literacy among the young \+ % of the population that is old \* literacy among the old. This way of thinking about it immediately allows us to decompose any changes in literacy into two components: The “share” component, which tells us how the % of the population in each category is changing over time, and the “age” component, which tells us the extent of literacy among the young and the old. For instance, if older people tend to be less literate and the population is becoming older over time, then literacy will fall, even if there is no change in literacy among the young or among the old. And in case there *are* these differences, we can go one step further and ask: If older people are less literate, is it because people tend to become less literate as they age, or is it because the older were educated at an earlier time, when perhaps educational attainment was a lot lower.
